About me

I'm an international student from Morocco, currently in my first year of Aerospace Engineering and Economics at NTU. Aerospace taught me to visualise complex systems before you can simplify them. Economics taught me how markets behave, how decisions get made under uncertainty.

Both together gave me a certain flexibility in how I think, and that's what drew me toward Applied Computing in Finance.

This year, participating in the NTU Women in Tech Ideathon gave me a clearer sense of where I actually want to go. Working on a solution around women's career re-entry, combining a shared transition fund with a structured re-entry pathway, I realised I was naturally doing three things at once: breaking down the problem, thinking about who would actually use this and how, and pushing for something concrete rather than just a concept.

Talking to students already in the ACF program confirmed it. What drew me wasn't just the technical side, it was the idea of being the person who sits between the data and the decision. That's what a product manager does. And that's what I want to build toward.

My current program gave me strong foundations, but I've reached a point where I know the direction I want to take, and Applied Computing in Finance is where that direction leads.

Why Applied Computing in Finance

From aerospace to finance, not a detour but a pattern

Aerospace engineering is fundamentally about constraint optimisation under uncertainty. So is portfolio management. So is financial product design. The switch isn't a pivot away from my training, it's applying the same rigorous systems thinking to a different domain.

The product manager thread

What draws me to Applied Computing in Finance specifically is the product layer. I don't just want to build models, I want to build tools that real users trust with real decisions. That's how these three projects were built: ship something, see it work, understand why.

Singapore as a lens on global finance

Being based in Singapore means operating in one of the most concentrated fintech ecosystems in the world. Singapore sits at the centre of Asia's financial flows, making it one of the most exposed hubs when geopolitical risk rises. That's not abstract to me, it's the context I'm living and studying in.

Halal finance as an underserved problem space

Islamic finance is one of the fastest-growing sectors in global finance, yet it remains massively underserved in terms of data tools, screening infrastructure, and product design. As someone who grew up Muslim and takes that seriously, I see this not just as a market opportunity but as a space where better technology can genuinely serve people who currently have very few options. A PM who understands both the technical and the cultural side of this problem is rare. That's something I can be.
